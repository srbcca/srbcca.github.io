In the Morning...

“O Lord, in the morning you hear my voice; in the morning I prepare a sacrifice for you and watch" (Psalms 5:3)."

My original goal was to somewhat form a better morning routine, but what ended up happening was while I would wake early to read, I would actually end up writing my posts at night. In practice, waking early, eating breakfast, walking my dog, and going through proper workday prep began to be pretty unmanageable and too task-oriented to be relaxing (so I have yet to find something that is reasonable that can also be replicated).

Not too long ago, I fell asleep exhausted at 10 and woke up at 5:30, unable to go back to sleep. An hour later I got out of bed, made breakfast, walked my dog, and got to work at a fairly reasonable time. During breakfast, I had to actively remind myself to take my time, enjoy it, and remember that there was no rush to get to work.

I found myself being reminded of the point of what I wanted: space to connect with the Lord while reading and a space to mull on thoughts by writing. First thing in the morning. While I'm a little bummed I fell off the bandwagon, I'm optimistic that I still agree with why I'd like to do this to begin with, and that successful morning is enough to get me started again.

I love this verse because of the word "watch." I'm in the thick of waiting on the Lord right now, in a very tangible sense but also as part of an overarching season of "what does God have for me?" and "what will things look like from here?" which have been questions honestly throughout my whole life.

I like that David specifically talks about an action/discipline that he does in the morning, meeting with God, waiting for God's response, and looking to see it. I'm inspired that God hears my voice, and my mood today is "watchful."

Jesus Brings the Fish

“Now there are also many other things that Jesus did. Were every one of them to be written, I suppose that the world itself could not contain the books that would be written” (John 21:25).

The disciples witnessed one of the most miraculous things in history - Jesus resurrecting and him being among their midst, and they're about to see him again a third time.

Yet, Simon goes fishing. Imagine returning to your craft after leaving it for what may have seemed like a grand adventure. They return to be fishermen. Simon decides to go fishing at night, and disciples join him, and they catch nothing.

In the morning, Jesus stands on the shore and tells the fishermen how to fish. He tells them to cast their net on the right side of the boat, and 153 fish appear in the net.

Then, Jesus has breakfast with them, recreating a charcoal fire after Peter threw himself into the water and swam to shore upon recognizing Jesus. Quite the moment - none of the disciples “dared” to ask him who he was: they knew.

Peter is asked three times if he truly loves Jesus, and is told to feed his sheep. He says to not look around at what people are doing, but to follow him!

The inspiration to keep working is here. It’s not just about catching fish. Living to work is not the point. Jesus says to follow him, he brings the fish.

Revelation is a Gift

“Having said this, she turned around and saw Jesus standing, but she did not know that it was Jesus” (John 20:14).

After Jesus is resurrected, he appears first to Mary. She for some reason is kept from initially recognizing him, and she mistakes him for the gardener… until he calls her name. In that moment, he reveals himself to her, and she recognizes him.

I can think of situations where my incorrect impression of someone was based off of a small thin slice that was unfair/inconsequential as a first impression. I can think of periods in my life when I have no reason why something is happening, but afterward, I’m able to look back with more clarity and more understanding.

I’m very interested in revelation, and how it is God’s doing (and his alone). Ultimately, what can we will ourselves to know or discover that God does not choose to reveal to us?

God allowed Mary to recognize him at the right time. The moment was just for Mary. I’m trusting he will do that again and I’m encouraged today by his timing.

17

For Ransom

“He who saw it has borne witness—his testimony is true, and he knows that he is telling the truth—that you also may believe” (John 19:35).

Jesus has just been betrayed, handed over by Pilate, and mockingly called the King of the Jews. He carried his cross, knowing the pain set before him, and was crucified. He was pieced in the side to witnesses to confirm his death, then bound with myrrh and aloes in linen and laid in a new tomb.

Jesus knew what was set before him and that he had to do this. He knew that the truth that he was the Son of God would lead to his death because people didn’t understand nor believe, yet authority did not belong to Pilate, but to God (11).

God planned for Jesus to die to ransom the very people that desired to kill him because he loved them that much. Despite their enslavement to sin, Christ died for them. He was the propitiation for their sins, once and for all, so they could be reconciled to God. Truly a beautiful and powerful name.

Going Into the World

“As you sent me into the world, so I have sent them into the world" (John 17:14, 18).

We’re very much in the world. Sometimes I feel like I’m not doing much in the world, let alone remember that I am sent as Jesus was sent.

How much do others care about my actions or why I do the things I do? I forget that I am sent with a message of truth (17).

To be in the world but not of it: our lives should have clear markable differences from others. It means that we are governed by different things and strive for different end results.

I’m challenged to review how my life looks the same (and that’s okay) and how my life looks different from those around me. I’m challenged to take a good look at what’s truly important to me in life. I’d like to remember constantly that life is much more than just the physical and here and now, and to be a good steward of what I have.

14

Joy That Can Never Be Stolen

"So also you have sorrow now, but I will see you again, and your hearts will rejoice, and no one will take your joy from you" (John 16:22).

Lasting joy can never be taken away if it is in Christ. Any kind of sorrow is temporary, even the deepest sorrows, but we are promised that our sorrow "will turn into joy." That is a bold promise that seems short of a miracle.

We are told to ask and we will receive so our joy may be full (24). My takeaway is that in my sorrows, I need to remember to just ask God in his name, and to wait on Him for joy.

13

Too Tired to Abide

"You did not choose me, but I chose you and appointed you that you should go and bear fruit and that your fruit should abide, so that whatever you ask the Father in my name, he may give to you. This things I command you, so that you will love one another" (John 15:16-17).

If I'm being honest, most of the time, I love myself the most. I serve myself and think of myself more than anyone else.

I recall a time in the last few years when I was more motivated to think of others and to sacrifice for them. I feel like I had more energy, was more willing (possible), and honestly had nothing to lose (but much to gain). I felt like my resources would never run out. The efforts honestly brought me joy and I never regretted becoming exhausted serving others. I wonder if deep down, the heart to do that is still there and has just been forgotten.

I've forgotten to some degree that I've been appointed, and that abiding in Jesus is necessary to bear fruit. And bearing fruit has the ultimate end of glorifying God, who is worthy of praise. And in that appointment, we are commanded to love each other. And loving each other sometimes means foregoing preferences, energy, time, plans, space, convenience, routine, or even doing something that I want to do.

I often have the thought that these days, I feel too selfish to love other people. Too tired and too uninspired and unmotivated to love other people. Is there room? I'm hoping that abiding (will be brooding more on abiding) will teach me moreso, and I'm guessing I'll have lots of opportunities to practice.

12

A Home & a Dwelling

Jesus answered him 'If anyone loves me, he will keep my word, and my Father will love him, and we will come to him and make our home with him" (14:23).

So many good things in this passage, but I'd like to focus on the idea of home, which has been a wonder to me since I left for college.

I would very much consider Austin my current home as much as Arlington was my childhood home and still holds special memories. Not only in the sense of my immediate family, but in the sense of a coming of age that is unlike any other stage of my life (though you could say that about Austin as well, or any stage of life if you're optimistic and open, I suppose).

There's a feeling of pride and ownership that I feel about Austin, and there's a cohesiveness that I feel in my living situation and more of a curation of belongings very much unlike the displacement that I've had almost every year within and after graduating college.

So what is a home? How immediate, tangible, environmental is a home, and what does it take?

To me, the word that sticks out is the work "make." It's something you make, which takes intention, It's something that someone may have made for you, which takes action and hard work. It likely would even evolve because I would think that the home you are making would continue to change as you change, and I would imagine that it grows more comprehensive and lovely and treasured because of a sense of ownership, communion, and belonging. And meals.

When Jesus says that he and God will make their home with those that love him/keep his commandments, it's definitely more than a physical location. More than just the body, but the spiritual as well: a family, belonging, an abiding, a governing, a dwelling. You're communing with Almighty God, your loving Father; your fellow heir Jesus who made the relationship possible; and the Spirit who dwells in us and intercedes for us.

Reminds me of this beautiful imagery, the best and most beautiful invitation I have ever heard:

"Behold, I stand at the door and knock. If anyone hears my voice and opens the door, I will come in to him and eat with him, and he with me" (Rev 3:20).

To Love & To Serve

"If I then, your Lord and Teacher, have washed your feet, you also ought to wash one another's feet" (13:14).

Something that has always intrigued me about the actions of a believer is the mark of true service. Serving each other and others, the humility and time and resources that go into it, and the humbleness that is required to pour yourself out for others again and again.

The joy in giving is really very special, and I miss the times when I felt like I had much more emotional energy to spend on serving others.

"By this all people will know that you are my disciples, if you have love for one another" (13:35).

Jesus has just finished washing the disciples feet, saying they are blessed if they not only know, but do as he does. He then proceeds to talk about how the world will know about him by how they love one another.

So what is loving, actually? Loving is not just serving, but it is in the very least serving. What we do know is that the act of intentionally loving is so powerful and so different from the norm and requires so much that it cannot help but bear witness to God.

The glory that comes from __

"Nevertheless, many even of the authorities believed in him, but for fear of the Pharisees they did not confess it, so that they would not be put out of the synagogue; for they loved the glory that comes from man more than the glory that comes from God" (12:43).

Belief in Jesus was growing after he raised Lazarus. Mary poured expensive perfume on his feet and wiped them with her hair. He's greeted with a crowd and palm branches in Jerusalem as he sits on a donkey, God speaks to him among a crowd inquiring about the Son of Man being lifted up... Jesus knows his time has come. Yet, still some did not believe. Some believed and did not confess it.

I've found myself asking, "Who do I live for?" And at times, I've really got to think about what my actions show me what I believe. Do I worship and serve myself first and overall without any regret or thought?

Recently, I've been thinking about what I use my resources for and I might want to invest them in. What happened to any dreams of living off less, giving much away, only owning and using what I need, and a lifestyle that might seem more different than I thought of where I am? I'd like for my heart to drive the way I live my life, and I desire that nothing would be lost in translation.

The Good Shepherd

"My sheep hear my voice, and I know them, and they follow me. I give them eternal life, and they will never perish, and no one will snatch them out of my hand" (John 10:28).

A few years ago, my friend H. and I were asked to read the Bible with a gal that was interested. At first, I believe we were hesitant. I didn't know if I felt ready, and we didn't know the gal, and other excuses that I really can't remember.

Our friend that connected us said "Snap out of it!" Or something more or less along those lines. He said we have found someone who wants to read the Bible. Will you do it? And we said yes.

Following was a couple of months where we would meet up and read stories: many parables, new testament, and the times were always pretty great. I remember feeling hesitant in myself and a lot of times, Helen would lead and ask the questions in a DBS-style study.

After a few months, we stopped and I may have thought that the gal had lost interest. But I heard later that she had actually started going to a different church, and had gotten baptized, and although I was only learning at the time, I couldn't help but appreciate to be even a part of that.

* * *

A few times, I feel like I've heard H. quote this very verse: Jesus' sheep recognize his voice when they hear it, so there is nothing to fear. And when they hear it, they will follow, because they're of his flock, and they know he is the good shepherd. He knows them by name, all of them, and lays down his life for the sheep, unlike a hired hand.

I really like this passage. I love that the shepherd calls all of his sheep by name, and they go in and out of him, the door, and find pasture. He's not just a shepherd, he's a good shepherd. He knows his sheep, and they know him. He comes so that they can have abundant life, and that is something that can never be lost.

John 9

Holistic verse:

"Jesus answered, 'It was not that this man sinned, or his parents, but that the works of God might be displayed in him.'"

Some Pharisees, trying to reason:

"How can a man who is a sinner do such signs?"

The parents of the healed man, perhaps in fear:

"Ask him; he is of age. He will speak for himself."

The man, with confidence and challenge:

"Why do you want to hear it again? Do you also want to become his disciples?

The man, then sure of his words:

"Why, this is an amazing thing! You do not know where he comes from, yet he opened my eyes. We know God does not listen to sinners, but if anyone is a worshiper of God and does his will, God listens to him. Never since the world began has it been heard that anyone opened the eyes of a man born blind. If this man were not from God, he could do nothing."

The Pharisees: aware of their compared knowledge and status:

"You were born in utter sin, and would you teach us?"

The man to Jesus, wanting to know who he's talking to:

"Who is he, sir, that I may believe in him?"

Jesus, revealing his cards after finding the man:

"You have seen him, and it is he who is speaking to you."

Jesus, on principles:

"For judgment I came into this world, that those who do not see may see, and those who see may become blind."

Words that come to mind: humbleness, to being open to being wrong and having someone else set you right. Those who doubted Jesus and as well as those who were curious/hesitant both needed Jesus to clarify exactly who he was.

Another word: truth. It is not enough for Jesus to be recognized as a "prophet" or miracle worker, but as the Son of God, recognized, believed, and worshiped. It is not enough for the man to be healed of his vision. It's important, but the story doesn't stop there. Jesus seeks him out to reveal himself as filling the right recipient of worship in his life, to sin no more, in line with helping those blind to actually see.

John 6

"Do not labor for the food that perishes, but for the food that endures to eternal life, which the Son of Man will give to you. For on him God the Father has set his seal" (27).

After Jesus feeds the 5,000, he retreats to the mountain after perceiving that the crowd would want to force him to be king.

That evening, he walks on water toward his frightened disciples in a boat. He reminds them who he is, and to not be afraid. They gladly take him in and surprisingly arrive across the sea.

The next day, he calls the crowd out for pursuing him because of the loaves of bread, not because of who he is. And since everyone's there because of the bread, he tells them there's a bread that comes from heaven. And no, it's better than the manna from history in the wilderness, it's beyond the physical.

More than that, the bread is a person - it's Jesus himself. The true bread from heaven, the bread of life that will be broken and raised up. And to have life, you must partake of it, which can be easily misconstrued. So much so that the Jews disputed and questioned who he was and some disciples walked away.

Better than giving the miraculous loaves or demonstrating mastery over water, Jesus provides a bold claim: himself, his presence, his words as the bread of life. He attests that there is more than the physical because even then, the Father "draws" those who come to Jesus (44) and the Spirit "gives life" whereas the flesh is no help at all (63).

As I drive on Mopac and rearrange my room and cook with friends, I contemplate: do I believe that Jesus is who he says he is--not merely the son of a couple named Joseph and Mary, but the bread of life, whose body and blood was sacrificed so we could live forever? I ask myself: am I living for more than the physical? To trust and take of Jesus is to bank my whole life on him, because he's much better than any other kind of bread.

I recognize the skepticism that fuels the question of if anything good could come from Nazareth (John 1:46), yet marvel at the beauty of that kind of lowly humbleness, and I see Peter's faith when he says "Lord, to whom shall we go?" Of course, it would be the Savior of the world, born in a barn.

Peter says that he has "come to know" that Jesus is the "Holy One of God" (68). The whole process of coming to know, in relationships and friendships and spiritually, seems like it consists of quite a bit, and it seems to be always surprising.

John 5

"You search the scriptures because you think that in them you have eternal life; and it is they that bear witness about me, yet you refuse to come to me that you may have life" (40).

When I was on a trip overseas years ago, some friends and I met a man that, upon receiving a tract, asked my friend for clarification on how to get to heaven. He then told us he was having dreams of Jesus and that he grew up Muslim, but when he read the Qu'ran, all he could see was that it prophesied about and pointed to Jesus as the way. He believed Jesus was God and in the virgin birth.

This was amazing to me and what followed was a crazy, awesome thing that I got to tangibly experience that is more personable told in person.

For a text to bear witness about the truth of Jesus to a person is a God-breathed mystery in which the Spirit is at work and Jesus speaks for himself. For a person to bear witness about Jesus in spoken word and testimony is such a gift and a wonder.

In John 5, Jesus asked a paralyzed man if he wanted to be healed. The man gave a few reasons why the pool wasn't going to work out, and with words, Jesus told him to get up, pick up his mat, and walk/go home. And after 38 years of paralysis, he did. But regarding his interesting original question of asking if he wanted to be healed: he told him to sin no more and from another account, that his said his sins were forgiven. It wasn't just a physical healing. And all witnesses of the miracle feared and glorified God.

I can attest that sometimes we get so caught up in doing things right that we forget about the actual people we are interacting with. We forget we're witnesses of some of the greatest miracles in our lives, and we forget so easily once they've happened. We can wait for so long for prayers to answered that we think we lose hope, and all it takes a word from Jesus for us to feel alive again.

May we not get so caught up in reading disciplines or major life milestones or schedules or __ while missing the point: encountering the person of Jesus.

John 4

"Jesus said to him, 'Go; your son will live.' The man believed the word that Jesus spoke to him and went on his way" (4:50).

I suspect I've forgotten the excitement of the unfolding of promises. When you're granted a "yes" or a promise, but have yet to see it come to fruition--you know what will happen. But how will it happen? Seems like the question of the season.

This man heard about Jesus and asked him to come and heal his dying son. He believed when Jesus said his son would live.

And as he headed back, he encountered his servants, who told him his son was recovering, and it began when Jesus said it would be so, and he connected the dots. And again, he believed in him (curious how this believing may be different from before). And then, his family believed.

Sometimes I want a sign, a coincidence, something spectacular, a green light, affirmation or complete divine confirmation first. And a few moments, I think I've gotten it.

But the other times, the unfolding is painful, and it requires hoping in God, and remembering end promises, and a tempering during the process. As we heard last Thursday evening, it is indeed, a season of waiting - in discipline, future, and prayers along the way.

John 3

"John answered, 'A person cannot receive even one thing unless it is given him from heaven.' (27)"

Something that I've greatly forgotten: everything I have I've received. Maybe I've asked for it, maybe I didn't even think to ask for it. Maybe I couldn't even have imagined to ask for it, or was too proud to ask for it, or was too faithless to keep asking for it, or didn't even know that asking was an option. But what I've received, someone decided it was good or right to give.

That means that my career, the favor I have in my relationships, or the settling and clarity of decisions I've made came didn't come purely from great decisions that I've made, or strong feelings that I've acted on, or any great skill or compassion or social practice, or whatever else I can try to take credit for. But God gave me everything in his right timing and great giving, and I believe he will continue to do that.

One thing I'd like to do: receive well. Recognize what I'm receiving, recognize that I can ask and should ask, and recognize that God gives. I'd like to be not too boastful or faithless to ask, and I'd like to ask knowing that it would be joyful to be said "yes" to and freely receive.
